## What this is

Word embeddings are dense, low-dimensional [[vectors]] that represent words as points in a continuous vector space. Each word is mapped to a real-valued vector (typically 100–768 dimensions) such that words appearing in similar contexts occupy nearby regions of the space.

## Why this matters

Traditional NLP represented words as one-hot vectors — sparse, high-dimensional, and semantically blind. Word embeddings solved three problems at once: they reduced dimensionality, captured semantic similarity, and enabled transfer learning by pre-training on large corpora. Every modern NLP system builds on this idea, even if the specific method has evolved from Word2Vec to contextual transformers.

## Core idea

The **distributional hypothesis** (Firth, 1957) states: "You shall know a word by the company it keeps." Word embeddings operationalise this by training a model to predict a word from its neighbours (or vice versa), then extracting the learned weight matrix as the embedding table. Words with similar contexts get similar vectors.

## Equation or formal logic

### Skip-gram objective (Word2Vec)

Given a centre word $w_c$ and a context word $w_o$, Skip-gram maximises:

$$
J = \frac{1}{T} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{\substack{-k \le j \le k \\ j \ne 0}} \log P(w_{t+j} \mid w_t)
$$

where $T$ is the corpus length and $k$ is the context window size.

The conditional probability is modelled with a softmax over the vocabulary $V$:

$$
P(w_o \mid w_c) = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{u}_{w_o}^\top \mathbf{v}_{w_c})}{\sum_{w=1}^{|V|} \exp(\mathbf{u}_w^\top \mathbf{v}_{w_c})}
$$

Here $\mathbf{v}_{w_c}$ is the input (centre) embedding and $\mathbf{u}_{w_o}$ is the output (context) embedding.

## Breakdown

### Terms

- $T$ — total number of tokens in the training corpus
- $k$ — context window radius (e.g., 5 words each side)
- $\mathbf{v}_{w}$ — the embedding vector for word $w$ (what we keep after training)
- $\mathbf{u}_{w}$ — the output weight vector for word $w$ (often discarded)
- $|V|$ — vocabulary size

### What the equation is really doing

The model learns to position each word's vector so that the dot product $\mathbf{u}_{w_o}^\top \mathbf{v}_{w_c}$ is high when $w_o$ genuinely appears near $w_c$ in real text, and low otherwise. After training, the input matrix $\mathbf{V}$ serves as the embedding table.

## Plain-English explanation

Imagine reading millions of sentences. You notice "doctor" often appears near "hospital", "patient", and "diagnosis", while "banana" appears near "fruit", "yellow", and "peel". An embedding model absorbs these co-occurrence patterns and assigns similar vectors to words that share contexts. After training, you can ask "which word is to *king* as *woman* is to *man*?" and the vector arithmetic $\mathbf{v}_{\text{king}} - \mathbf{v}_{\text{man}} + \mathbf{v}_{\text{woman}}$ lands near $\mathbf{v}_{\text{queen}}$.

## Worked example

**Classic analogy test:**

$$
\mathbf{v}_{\text{king}} - \mathbf{v}_{\text{man}} + \mathbf{v}_{\text{woman}} \approx \mathbf{v}_{\text{queen}}
$$

Using pretrained 300-d GloVe vectors, the nearest neighbour of the result vector is indeed "queen" with [[cosine_similarity]] > 0.85.

**Biomedical example:**

In embeddings trained on PubMed abstracts, $\mathbf{v}_{\text{aspirin}}$ is close to $\mathbf{v}_{\text{ibuprofen}}$ and $\mathbf{v}_{\text{NSAID}}$, but distant from $\mathbf{v}_{\text{penicillin}}$. This emergent clustering enables drug-class discovery without manual ontology curation.

## Why this is used

1. **Semantic similarity** — [[cosine_similarity]] between embeddings correlates with human similarity judgements.
2. **Transfer learning** — embeddings pretrained on large corpora improve downstream tasks with limited labelled data.
3. **Dimensionality reduction** — from $|V|$-dimensional one-hot to $d$-dimensional dense, enabling efficient computation.
4. **Feature input** — embeddings serve as the first layer of nearly every neural NLP model.

## Benefits

* Captures semantic and syntactic regularities (analogies, plurals, tense).
* Pretrained embeddings are freely available (GloVe, fastText, BioWordVec) and easy to integrate.
* Low storage: 1M words × 300 dims = ~1.2 GB in float32.
* Enables unsupervised discovery of concept clusters in domain-specific corpora.

## Drawbacks and failure modes

* **Polysemy blindness** — static embeddings assign one vector per word; "bank" (river) and "bank" (finance) share the same vector. Contextual models (BERT, GPT) address this.
* **Bias amplification** — embeddings absorb societal biases present in training data (gender, racial stereotypes).
* **Out-of-vocabulary problem** — words unseen during training have no embedding. Subword models (fastText, BPE) mitigate this.
* **Static representations** — do not adapt to sentence-level context, limiting performance on tasks requiring disambiguation.
* **Training corpus dependency** — embeddings trained on Wikipedia perform poorly on biomedical text, and vice versa; domain match matters.
